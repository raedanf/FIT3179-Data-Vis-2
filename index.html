Population and Economic Comparisons in Australian States and Territories

Raedan Fernandez

Australia is divided into 6 States and 2 Territories. These areas differ greatly in Size, Population and Economic status. This visualisation will explore the differences between the States and Territories and how Australia has evolved over time.

Population Demographics

Source: ABS

Although the populations in each region can range from a few hundred thousand to multiple millions, the proportion of sexes in each region is generally consistent across each state and territory.

Looking at the spread of population in terms of age instead of region shows that although the proportion of Males and Females is very similar for younger age groups, in the more senior groups, women far outnumber men. The majority of people in Australia are millenials, with the population decreasing in older and younger age groups.

Source: ABS

Population Spread

Source: Geoscience Australia

Source: ABS

The differences in populations and sizes of regions in Australia is most evident in this plot. As the chart on the left shows, regions such as the ACT are incredibly dense with around 200 people per square kilometer. Conversely, the Northern Territory is sparsely populated, having less than 1 person for every 5 square kilometers.

Using a log scale for the populations of each region, we can better see the trends in the state populations. Generally the populations of states and territories grow consistently, with the only exceptions to this trend being the population of the ACT slowly approaching the population of Tasmania and Victoria's population having a slight reduction during the pandemic; possibly due to lockdowns.

Source: ABS

Economic Status

Source: Simple Maps

Source: ABS

The majority of Australia's GDP comes from the Eastern and Western states, with the central regions having much lower GSPs. This is also affected by the populations of each region, as a more populated region will generally have a higher GSP. When looking at GSPs per capita, it becomes clear that Western Australia and the Northern Territory have the highest GSPs when, adjusting for their population size.

Each Australian State and Territory is unique, with each region varying greatly in size, population demographics and economic output.

Over time, with the country progressing and changing at its current rate, the demographics and populations of each region will continue to provide interesting trends and patterns to analyse.
